SARSA results:
The SARSA agent was able to learn a useful policy. 
During evaluation, the reward improved from 9.0 at the beginning to values such as 50.0 and sometimes 90.0. 
The final evaluation reward was 50.0.

Q-learning results:
The Q-learning agent also learned a useful policy. 
Its evaluation reward also improved from 9.0 at the beginning to values such as 50.0 and sometimes 90.0. 
The final evaluation reward was also 50.0.

Comparison:
SARSA and Q-learning can sometimes reach similar scores as last week's methods, for example 50.0 or 90.0, 
but the evaluation rewards are not perfectly stable. 

This is likely because the agents are model-free and learn only from sampled interactions with the environment. 
Unlike value iteration or policy iteration, they do not know the full transition model and reward model.

SARSA updates Q-values using the next action that is actually selected by the current epsilon-greedy policy. 

Q-learning updates Q-values using the best possible next action, max_a Q(s', a), independent of the action that is actually selected during exploration.

In my runs, both algorithms reached similar final performance. 
I did not observe a very strong difference in the final reward, but Q-learning sometimes reached high rewards slightly earlier. 
However, because only one evaluation episode was used, the results can fluctuate a lot.

One interesting observation is that the reward sometimes suddenly drops to 0.0, even after many training steps(14000 steps).
I think this happens because one unlucky episode can produce a reward of 0.0.
Also, the learned policy may still not be fully stable, which means the agent can perform well in many cases, but it may still choose a bad action in some states.
